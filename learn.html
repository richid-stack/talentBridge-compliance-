What is the EU AI Act?

The EU AI Act is the world's first comprehensive law for artificial intelligence. It classifies AI systems by risk level and puts strict rules on high-risk AI starting August 2, 2026. The goal is to protect people while encouraging safe innovation.

Risk Levels Explained

Prohibited (Unacceptable Risk) — Banned completely (e.g., social scoring systems, real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces).

High-Risk (Annex III) — Strict obligations. This is where most remote AI jobs fall: employment AI, credit scoring, education tools, biometrics, critical infrastructure. Deployers must prove human oversight, data quality, logging, and bias checks.

Limited Risk — Only transparency required (e.g., chatbots must say they are AI).

Minimal Risk — No obligations (spam filters, video games).

Article 26 – Meaningful Human Oversight (Most Important for You)

For high-risk AI, deployers must have trained humans who can:

  • Understand how the AI makes decisions
  • Intervene, override, or stop the AI in real time
  • Monitor for errors or bias

GDPR & AI – What You Must Know

GDPR still applies whenever AI processes personal data. Key rules:

  • Lawful processing, data minimization, security
  • Right to access, erasure ("right to be forgotten"), and explanation of AI decisions
  • Strict rules on international data transfers (Africa → EU)

Red-Teaming – How to Test & Break AI Safely

Red-Teaming means trying to "break" AI systems before they go live. You test for:

  • Bias (does it discriminate based on race/gender?)
  • Hallucinations (wrong or made-up answers)
  • Safety failures (can it give harmful advice?)
  • Manipulation (can users trick it?)
